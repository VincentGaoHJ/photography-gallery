## Beyond the Golden Hour

We all know about golden hour. That magical window just after sunrise and just before sunset when the world is bathed in warm, directional light. It is beautiful. It is also predictable.

What interests me more is finding extraordinary light in ordinary places. The reflected light off a white building at midday. The shafts of light cutting through a dusty window. The neon glow of a city street after rain.

## The Parking Garage Epiphany

Some of my favorite editorial work was shot in a parking garage. Yes, a parking garage.

The concrete floors act as massive reflectors, bouncing light upward in the most flattering way. The open sides create a natural softbox effect. And the geometric repetition of columns and ramps provides a structured backdrop that contrasts beautifully with organic human forms.

## Working with What You Have

The key to seeing light is to stop looking for "good light" and start observing how light actually behaves. Every space has light. The question is what kind, and what mood it creates.

- **Hard light** from a single bare window creates drama and contrast
- **Overcast skies** act as a giant softbox, perfect for skin tones
- **Mixed color temperatures** (window light + tungsten) create visual tension
- **Reflected light** bouncing off colored walls picks up unexpected hues

## A Practical Exercise

When I teach workshops, I give students this assignment:

Go to a single room. Don't leave it for an hour. Photograph only what the light allows. No flashes. No modifiers. Just observation.

The results are always surprising. Light is everywhere. You just have to slow down enough to see it.
