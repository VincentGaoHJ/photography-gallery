## The Power of Black and White

There is something undeniably powerful about a black and white portrait. Without the distraction of color, the viewer is forced to confront the raw emotion in the subject's eyes, the texture of their skin, the play of light across their features.

I have always been drawn to monochrome work. Perhaps it is the way shadows become more pronounced, or how highlights take on an almost ethereal quality. Whatever the reason, some of my most intimate portraits have been captured without color.

## Light First, Everything Else Second

When I approach a monochrome portrait session, my entire focus shifts to light. Color no longer does the heavy lifting of creating mood — that responsibility falls entirely on how light sculpts the subject.

I often work with a single key light, positioned slightly above eye level and at a 45-degree angle. This creates a classic Rembrandt pattern that introduces depth without becoming overly dramatic.

> "Black and white creates a strange dreamscape that color never can."

## The Technical Side

For those interested in the technical details:

- **Camera:** Medium format digital, 100MP
- **Lens:** 80mm f/2.8 — the compression at this focal length is perfect for portraits
- **Lighting:** Profoto B10 with a 3-foot octabox
- **Post:** Capture One for RAW processing, Photoshop for final retouching

## Finding Connection

The technical specifications matter, but what truly makes a portrait sing is the connection between photographer and subject. Before I even pick up the camera, I spend at least fifteen minutes talking with my subject. No camera. No lights. Just conversation.

This ritual has become the most important part of my process. By the time I raise the viewfinder to my eye, the subject has forgotten to be self-conscious. They are simply present.

That presence is what I chase in every frame.
